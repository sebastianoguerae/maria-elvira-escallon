Biography

Maria Elvira
Escallon

Maria Elvira Escallon does not illustrate violence. She points. She does not criticize — she reveals.

Born in London in 1954 and initially trained as a psychologist, Escallon has spent more than four decades building a body of work situated at the frontier between history and memory. Her practice articulates sculptural processes, photography and in-situ work to reveal a present threatened by historical failures, establishing thresholds that connect times and fragments of stories through elements of material culture.

Her interventions — sometimes subtle and imperceptible, sometimes monumental — take place in abandoned hospitals, ruined train stations, native trees, or buildings marked by violence. Deterioration is not her subject: it is her material. Dust, melting ice, bark reclaiming a baroque carving — each work makes time visible as a sculptural phenomenon.

She belongs to a generation of Colombian artists who work with the memory of conflict without being literal — alongside figures like Doris Salcedo, Oscar Munoz, and Clemencia Echeverri — but her voice is distinct: quieter, more documentary, closer to the archive than to the gesture.
