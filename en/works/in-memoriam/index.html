Work / 003

In Memoriam

2001  /  Ephemeral sculpture — ice, glass, water  /  300 kg, duration 20 hours  /  Alianza Colombo Francesa, Bogota

A 300-kilogram ice column dissolving slowly in water over twenty hours inside a glass container. The work captures the passage of time as irreversible material loss, making duration visible as a sculptural phenomenon. The viewer witnesses the transformation — from solid monument to liquid absence. Nothing remains.

The process of dissolution is the work itself. There is no privileged moment of contemplation: each instant is equally valid and simultaneously ephemeral. The ice column functions as a clock that measures its own disappearance, a monument that consumes itself in the very act of existing.

The glass container operates as both display case and receptacle — it exhibits the sculpture while collecting its remains. At the end of the twenty hours, what was once a monumental column is only water: the form has disappeared completely, but the matter remains transformed. The transparency of the glass makes every stage of this metamorphosis visible.

CONTEXT

This ephemeral sculpture embodies Escallon's interest in temporal processes and the vulnerability of materials. The ice column functions as both monument and anti-monument — its grandeur resides precisely in its impermanence.

In Memoriam belongs to a tradition of process-based sculpture that includes Joseph Beuys's interventions with fat and felt as well as Andy Goldsworthy's ice sculptures. However, Escallon's work distinguishes itself through its specific symbolic charge: in the Colombian context of 2001, the dissolution of a monument acquires inevitable political and social resonances — the fragility of institutions, the loss of collective memory, the impermanence of everything that has been built.

The work anticipates Escallon's central concern with temporality as sculptural material, a theme she would develop with greater complexity in Nuevas Floras (where time transforms the intervention over the course of years) and in En estado de coma (where she documents the slow dissolution of a heritage building).
