En estado de coma

2005–2007  /  5 photographic series, video, sculptural installation  /  Hospital San Juan de Dios, Bogota

[ Pending: En estado de coma — General view of Hospital San Juan de Dios ]
[ Pending: En estado de coma — Postcards series ]

The project began in 2005 as a documentary seeking to make visible the conditions of Hospital San Juan de Dios — founded in 1564, outstanding for its level of specialization — after falling into abandonment. It includes five photographic series: Postcards, Nocturnal Walks, Soft Tissue, Extractions, and Cultures.

The hospital was closed in 2001 as a consequence of mismanagement and the privatization of the healthcare system. A small group of employees continued giving 'oxygen to the terminal patient.' The title of the work refers to 'an entity that is not dead and about which there is hope.'

Escallon did not document the hospital as a picturesque ruin but as an organism in a state of suspension. The five series address different facets of this liminal condition: the inverted tourist postcards, the nocturnal walks that reveal what daylight conceals, the soft tissue of sheets still covering empty beds, the extractions of architectural elements as if they were clinical samples, and the cultures of vegetation reclaiming the abandoned spaces.

SERIES

  • Postcards — Photographs of the hospital treated as tourist postcards, inverting the promotional logic to reveal deterioration.
  • Nocturnal Walks — Photographic record of the hospital corridors and wards at night, when absence becomes more palpable.
  • Soft Tissue — Documentation of the sheets, curtains, and hospital textiles that remain as vestiges of medical activity.
  • Extractions — Architectural and hospital elements isolated from their context, treated as samples from a sick body.
  • Cultures — Record of the vegetation that invades and reclaims the abandoned spaces of the hospital.

CONTEXT

Hospital San Juan de Dios, founded in 1564, was one of Colombia's most important public hospitals, providing care to the poorest. After its closure in 2001, it was declared national cultural heritage in 2002. The building, designed by architect Pablo de la Cruz, represents a central chapter in the history of Colombian public health.

Escallon's work is part of her line of research on heritage in abandonment and the relationship between the State and the buildings that embody collective memory. As in In Vitro, the artist works with a heritage building that the State has left in ruins, but here the scale is monumental and the deterioration extends over years.

The project was published as a book by the IDPC and Johns Hopkins University Press (Project MUSE), consolidating its importance both in the artistic field and in the discussion on cultural heritage and public health in Colombia.
