Work / 004

Desde Adentro

2003  /  12 C-type photographs, 12-hour video installation  /  89 x 123 cm (photographs), edition of 5

Five days after the terrorist attack on Club El Nogal in February 2003, Escallon — who served as the club's Cultural Director — documented the aftermath in 12 photographs and a 12-hour video. The traces of absent people and missing objects transport the viewer to lived moments, despite arriving 'late to the scene of facts.'

The photographs capture impressions found on surfaces and everyday objects — doors, walls, stairs — preserving the atmosphere of destruction and the memory of the incident. The film of ash that covered everything became a register of absence. The traces function as indexes of what is no longer there: handprints, the outlines of removed paintings, the silhouettes of volatilized objects.

The 12-hour video installation slowly traverses the devastated spaces, constructing a time parallel to that of the destruction. The camera lingers on details that the everyday gaze would overlook, turning the document into a meditation on the fragility of the spaces we inhabit.

CONTEXT

On February 7, 2003, a car bomb exploded inside Club El Nogal in northern Bogota, killing 36 people and injuring more than 200. The attack was attributed to the FARC and deeply shook Colombian society. Escallon's documentation became one of the most significant artistic responses to urban violence in Colombian contemporary art.

Escallon's singular position — as both the club's Cultural Director and a visual artist — gave her unique access to the scene of the attack. Her documentation operates at the intersection between forensic record and artistic gaze, transforming the evidence of destruction into a reflection on loss, memory, and the trace as a form of presence.

Desde Adentro consolidated Escallon's practice around the arrival 'late to the scene of facts' — a position that would recur in later works such as En estado de coma — where the artist does not document the event itself but its consequences, the traces that remain when everything else has disappeared.
