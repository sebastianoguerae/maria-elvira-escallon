Encuentros con Seres Notables · Full video · 2:00 min

The title is inspired by the Russian mystic George Gurdjieff's book Meetings with Remarkable Men. The exhibition explores kinship between humans and other living beings — trees and plants — proposing an encounter that dissolves the boundaries between the human body and the vegetal world.

Escallon discovered branches that bear an uncanny resemblance to human skeletal anatomy — arms, joints, the articulations of a body. These found forms become the basis for a series of sculptures in which the distinction between the organic structures of trees and those of the human body becomes impossible to maintain. The branches are not carved or manipulated to look human; they simply do, revealing a morphological kinship that is both scientific and poetic.

The series includes sub-works such as Brazo and Desclavada, each developing specific aspects of this human-vegetal correspondence. The use of eucalyptus and clay alongside the found branches creates a material vocabulary that bridges the natural and the sculptural, the discovered and the composed.

CONTEXT

Gurdjieff's Meetings with Remarkable Men recounts the author's spiritual journeys and encounters with teachers who transformed his understanding of the world. Escallon's appropriation of the title reframes the 'remarkable beings' as trees and plants — shifting the encounter from the human-spiritual to the inter-species, proposing that the most transformative meetings may be those between humans and the other living forms with which we share the world.

The work extends Escallon's longstanding engagement with the vegetal world, begun in Nuevas Floras (2003), but here the relationship is inverted: rather than imposing human forms onto trees, the artist discovers human forms already present within them.
