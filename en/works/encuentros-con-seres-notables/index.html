The title is inspired by the book by the Russian mystic George Gurdjieff, "Meetings with Remarkable Men," reimagined to celebrate connections with nature. The exhibition explores the kinship between human beings and other living beings — trees and plants — highlighting sensitive aspects that reveal great similarities among living beings.

Escallon discovered branches in the forest that resemble human skeletal anatomy: femurs, humeri, scapulae, radii and ulnae, phalanges. Through careful carving, she revealed forms where "their two branches stretch out their fingers trying to touch each other," creating visual echoes of Michelangelo's Creation scene.

The exhibition includes sculptures made from carved branches that resemble human bones, suggesting a structural correspondence between plant and animal organisms. The materials include sand, clay in various states (fresh and fired), and pieces shaped by water and sea sand.

CONTEXT

Escallon researched hybrids between nature and humans in art history, finding that hybrids between humans and plants are scarce — the mandrake, Daphne, Tolkien's Ents. This scarcity motivated a deep exploration of the structural correspondences between the vegetal and the animal, revealing kinships that normally go unnoticed.
